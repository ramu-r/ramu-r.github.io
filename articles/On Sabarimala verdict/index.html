The complexities of the Sabarimala verdict

We would have to step outside the false dichotomy between a progressive constitutionalism and a conservative religion to understand the many nuances of the Sabarimala issue.

Law vs religion

Under a constitutionalism that harmonises law and religion, It would be an uncharitable interpretation to consider the law as opposed to religion. The constitution recognizes religious practices are to be taken on faith and not examined through the lens of constitutional reason. Thus the judiciary strives to protect religion by giving priority to Article 26 over other fundamental rights like Article 14 leading to the protection of religious practices, even those derogatory of fundamental rights.

The triple talaq is a prominent example where the judiciary held instant talaq was invalid not because it violated gender equality, but because it was not an essential part of Islam. In other words, had the practice been an essential part of Islam, it would have been upheld despite being inimical to gender equality.

Religous custom vs social custom

But this protection is limited to religious customs and not social customs, a distinction not often clear in practice. It is through the closer reading of religious scriptures that the judiciary ascertains whether a practice forms an essential part of religious custom.

This question was central to the Sabarimala verdict and formed the basis for Indu Malhotra’s dissenting note. The verdict held exclusion of women as a shrine specific custom and not an essential practice in Ayyappa worship or Hinduism while the dissenting note argues Sabarimala worship should be considered as a separate sect in itself, one where the exclusion of women was an essential practice.

Hinduisation of local customs

Even a cursory survey of Ayyappa worship would show us the relevance of the dissenting note and how the judiciary is ill equipped to fully consider the diversity of Hindu worship. Ayyappa it seems is worshiped in shrine specific forms all over the state. He is worshiped as a child in Kolathupuzha, Kerala; as a young boy in Aryankavu, Kerala; as a grhasthya (householder) in Achankovil, Kerala; In Sabarimala, he is worshiped as a brahmachari, with rituals appropriate to a brahmachari.

But as far as the courts are concerned, the practices at Sabarimala will have to find sanction within Hindu scripture to avail protection for despite being a temple visited by people of all religions. It has long been argued the rise of militant Hindutva is due to the homogenisation and flattening of cultural practices by the Indian state and Sabarimala stands prime example.

An Unreasonable progressiveness

While the verdict was celebrated by the progressive media and the words of judge Chandrachood quoted line by line and shared tweet by tweet, it is this very progressiveness that forms the weakest link in the verdict’s line of reasoning.

Comparing entry of women with Sati or untouchability is misleading and devoid of nuance. The temple where women are already allowed does not discriminate against a gender or class as a whole but restricts based on age. Furthermore, The exclusion of a particular subset of women to one particular temple being compared with practices that held sway over every section of society for centuries was specious at best.

The judiciary might call for interpreting the constituion in a progressive light but in the country where it is implemented, this progressiveness is a candidate in search of a constitutency. Arguably, there might not even be a panchayat where progressive values will win against conservatism. The top down implementation of progressiveness in such a society is unreasonable and should be held as against the will of the people from which the constitution derives it’s authority. The law directed against the masses has not the nature of a reasonable institution, and neither has it the authority of a reasonable institution. The conservative backlash that ensued shows us all this was more than self evident to the protesting layperson which unfortunately was not apparent to the propounders of transformative constitutionalism.

While it can always be argued the backlash was from certain minority sections who opposed the verdict, the overzealous nature of state action and the undue use of force lend credance to the argument it was not just a minority, but the masses as a whole opposing the implementation of the verdict. Might it be due to his Stalinist roots, on an issue of crucial social import, chief minister Pinarayi Vijayan forgot that justice should not only be done but also seems to be done.
