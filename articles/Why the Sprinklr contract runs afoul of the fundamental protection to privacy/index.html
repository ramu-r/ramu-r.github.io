Why the Sprinklr contract runs afoul of the fundamental protection to privacy

While the government argues the Sprinklr deal is a legitimate action, a closer reading of the law says there’s no legal backing for it.

Privacy became a fundamental right through the Puttuswamy verdict where it says every individual may have control over the dissemination of material that is personal to him. If the state wants to grant Sprinklr access to patient information, the state needs informed consent from the patients about what Sprinklr is going to do with their information.

So the first question is, Has the government gotten informed consent from the patients about giving their data to Sprinklr? Not as far as we kow.

We aren’t arguing here privacy is an absolute right. The government is allowed to constrain this right within reasonable limits, but privacy arises from right to life in Article 21 and can’t be taken away without ‘procedure established by law’. If the government want to restrict the right it can only be on the basis of some legislation that allows this and not simply an executive government order, or a secretary’s discretion.

As far as I understand, the health related legislation in Kerala are mainly 3 British era laws, The Epidemics Act, The Madras Health Act and The Travancore Health Act, none of which allows the governments to disclose private health data. Here when the government is breaching privacy of health records, it is doing so without any legal basis.

That leads us to the second question, Which law backs this sort of intrusion.

An argument could be made the Epidemics Act and the NDMA Act contains provisions that give blanket permissions to the government, provisions that say the government can take whatever steps necessary to contain an epidemic.

Even if we are to take this argument at face value, the judiciary has prescribed limits there as well.

In judgments such as Puttaswamy and Anuradha Bhasin, it has been held that this government action must be proportionate to the effect it intends to have.

This test for proportionality has been laid out in Modern Dental College and Research Centre v. State of Madhya Pradesh that said any action is proportionate only if fulfills the following reasons

  1. Is it for the right purpose
  2. Will it actually give the intended outcome
  3. Are there any better alternative measures
  4. Is there any need to get this outcome at this cost

And the government hasn’t answered any of these questions. Nobody knows why we need the dashboard, nobody knows what we are going to achieve through the dashboard, and nobody, not the health workers, not the patients, knows what is being done with their data.

So the third questions is, what is it that we are expecting to gain from this dashboard, why do we need this, and what is being done with the dashboard now

One final argument we may have to face is, it would be argued none of this applies to data being given to Sprinklr as it is working as a wing of the state at this point. All of this applies to giving data to anyone, including the state, and not just the public. Justice Chandrachud makes this amply clear when he wrote with great foresight..

“…the state may assert a legitimate interest in analysing data borne from hospital records to understand and deal with a public health epidemic such as malaria or dengue to obviate a serious impact on the population”

Even the government going through health records and creating an enumerated list would would need to fulfil these requirements. This might seem non intuitive at first but the legal vulgarity of it becomes apparent if we imagine a state making lists of people with Leprosy, or maybe even Muslims, where religion is equivalently private data. And Chandrachud goes on to say that unless the state protects the anonymity of the citizens it will have to show the public interest behind it, which leads us back to the proportionality test.

Here while the state impinges upon our right to privacy, and a long list of other basic guarantees like the right to a life with dignity, we might have an impulse to justify state action because of the pandemic but we should remember this isn’t what civilized states do. If the government could pass an ordinance to increase the fines under the Epidemic Act, it could very well have found some legal basis for it’s actions unless it wanted to hide something. And all this was why the IT Secretary should have had legal advice before signing the contract.
