Why the Sprinklr contract runs afoul of the fundamental protection to privacy

While the government argues the Sprinklr deal is a legitimate action, a closer reading of the law says there’s no legal backing for giving out health data.

Note: This article was published before the interim order of the Kerala High Court on the 24th April and raises some questions the government has answered before the court. But the article has been left unchanged

Privacy became a fundamental right through the Puttuswamy verdict where it says every individual may have control over the dissemination of material that is personal to him. If the state wants to grant Sprinklr access to patient information, the state needs informed consent from the patients about what Sprinklr is going to do with their information.

So the first question is, Does the government have the patient’s informed consent about giving their data to Sprinklr? Not as far as we know.

The intention isn’t to argue privacy is an absolute right. The government can constrain this right within reasonable limits, but privacy arises from the right to life in Article 21, which can’t be taken away without a ‘procedure established by law’. If the government wants to restrict the right it can only be on the basis of some legislation that allows this and not simply an executive government order, or at a secretary’s discretion.

As far as it’s understood the health related legislation in Kerala are mainly 3 British era laws, the Epidemics Act, the Madras Public Health Act and the Travancore Cochin Public Health Act, none of which allows the governments to disclose private health data. Here when the government is breaching privacy of health records, it is doing so without any legal basis.

That leads us to the second question, What law backs this sort of intrusion.

An argument could be made the Epidemics Act and the Disaster Management Act contains provisions that give blanket permissions to the government, provisions that say the government may take whatever steps necessary to contain an epidemic.

Even if we are to take this argument at face value, the judiciary has prescribed limits there as well.

In judgments such as Puttaswamy and Anuradha Bhasin, it has been held government action that infringes on privacy must be proportionate to the effect it intends to have.

This test for proportionality has been laid out in Modern Dental College and Research Centre v. State of Madhya Pradesh that said any action is proportionate only if fulfills the following conditions

  1. Is it for the right purpose
  2. Will it actually give the intended outcome
  3. Are there any better alternative measures
  4. Do we really need to have this outcome at this cost

The government hasn’t answered any of these questions. Nobody knows why we need the dashboard, nobody knows what we are going to achieve through the dashboard, and nobody, not the health workers, not the patients, knows what is being done with their data.

And the third question is, what is it that we are expecting from this dashboard, why do we need this, and what is being done with the dashboard data now

One final argument the government could make is that none of this applies to data being given to Sprinklr, that Sprinklr is working as a wing of the state at this point. Contrary to that, all of this applies to giving data to anyone, including the state, and not just the public. Justice Chandrachud makes this amply clear when he wrote with great foresight..

“…the state may assert a legitimate interest in analysing data borne from hospital records to understand and deal with a public health epidemic such as malaria or dengue to obviate a serious impact on the population. If the State preserves the anonymity of the individual it could legitimately assert a valid state interest in the preservation of public health to design appropriate policy interventions on the basis of the data available to it. “

Even if the government going through health records and creating an enumerated list, it would would need to fulfill these requirements. The state has to protect the anonymity of the citizens and show legitimate public interest behind it, which leads us back to the proportionality test.

Even while the state impinges upon our right to privacy, and a long list of other basic guarantees like the right to a life with dignity, we might have an impulse to justify state action because of the pandemic but we should remember this isn’t what civilized states do. If the government could pass an ordinance to increase the fines under the Epidemic Act, it could very well have found some legal basis for it’s actions unless it wanted to hide something.
